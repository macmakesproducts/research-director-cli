## Executive summary

The consolidated picture from AHA/ACC guidelines, the ACC Clinical Practice Algorithm, and multiple natural-history cohorts is that small VSDs in asymptomatic toddlers are managed with surveillance, not intervention — and a non-trivial fraction continue to close spontaneously well past age 2. Intervention in a small VSD becomes justified only with specific triggers: a hemodynamically significant shunt (Qp:Qs ≥1.5–2 with left-ventricular volume overload), aortic valve prolapse or progressive aortic regurgitation (especially in perimembranous or doubly-committed defects), a history of endocarditis, or pulmonary hypertension that is still reversible. The mortality of elective repair is under 1% at experienced centers, but that statistic applies to defects that warrant repair; the appropriate counter-question for a 'small' VSD is what specific anatomic or functional finding is driving the recommendation. Endocarditis prophylaxis is no longer routinely indicated for unrepaired VSDs, and most children with small VSDs have no activity restrictions and normal life expectancy. Push back is reasonable any time intervention is offered without a documented size, location, shunt fraction, chamber-size, or valve-involvement justification.

## Key findings

- *Small, asymptomatic VSDs in toddlers are managed by observation, not intervention. Surveillance is the standard for small or moderate defects without hemodynamic compromise, with cardiology checkups whose frequency decreases as the child gets older — small muscular VSDs can be followed every 2–5 years and may even be discharged to primary care.* Sources: Cincinnati Children's, American College of Cardiology Clinical Practice Algorithm, StatPearls (NCBI). Confidence: high — Consistent across the ACC algorithm, a major academic children's hospital, and a peer-reviewed reference text.
- *Spontaneous closure continues past age 2. Although closure is most common in the first year, cumulative closure rates rise from roughly 24% at 18 months to 50% at 4 years and 75% by age 10 for small defects, and muscular VSDs close at notably higher rates than perimembranous ones. Johns Hopkins specifically notes that some VSDs don't close until age 4.* Sources: Baylor University Medical Center Proceedings review (Zhang et al.), Moe & Guntheroth ten-year follow-up (PubMed), Johns Hopkins Medicine. Confidence: high — Multiple independent cohort studies and review articles converge on similar trajectories, though absolute rates vary by population and defect type.
- *Guideline-based indications for closing a VSD are specific and narrow: a Qp:Qs ≥2 with left-ventricular volume overload (or Qp:Qs >1.5 with LV systolic/diastolic dysfunction), a history of endocarditis, progressive aortic regurgitation or aortic valve cusp prolapse, or symptomatic pulmonary overcirculation. Pulmonary artery pressure and pulmonary vascular resistance must remain <2/3 systemic for closure to be recommended.* Sources: StatPearls citing 2008 ACC/AHA guidelines, Medscape (AHA/ACC summary), ACC Clinical Practice Algorithm 2023. Confidence: high — Directly drawn from published AHA/ACC guideline criteria and a peer-reviewed clinical algorithm.
- *An aortic valve cusp prolapse is one of the few situations where a small VSD warrants surgery, because early repair can prevent progression of aortic insufficiency. This is especially relevant for perimembranous and doubly-committed subarterial VSDs, which are anatomically close to the aortic valve.* Sources: Medscape (Ventricular Septal Defects Treatment & Management), Springer Current Treatment Options in Cardiovascular Medicine. Confidence: high — Consistent across two clinical references; reflects a long-standing exception in pediatric cardiology practice.
- *Routine endocarditis antibiotic prophylaxis is NOT recommended for unrepaired VSDs under current AHA guidance. Prophylaxis is reserved for cyanotic CHD, prosthetic material, prior endocarditis, or the first 6 months after repair. Good oral hygiene is the primary preventive measure; however, lifetime endocarditis risk for unrepaired VSDs is non-zero and some authors argue current guidelines may underprotect this group.* Sources: American Heart Association, StatPearls (NCBI), Pediatric Heart Specialists. Confidence: high — Multiple authoritative sources agree on current AHA recommendations; the dissenting view about residual risk is openly acknowledged in the literature.
- *Elective VSD repair in North America carries a mortality rate below 1% with most patients achieving normal lifespan — but this favorable statistic applies to defects with valid indications for repair. Risks of surgery still include conduction abnormalities (heart block requiring pacemaker), residual shunt, and rare arrhythmias. Transcatheter closure is FDA-approved only for muscular VSDs in the U.S. and carries a particular conduction-disturbance risk for perimembranous defects.* Sources: StatPearls (NCBI), JACC Focus Seminar on Transcatheter Closure, ScienceDirect (Indications and Outcomes). Confidence: high — Consistent across peer-reviewed surgical outcome reviews and JACC.
- *Children with small VSDs typically have no activity restrictions, grow and develop normally, and live healthy lives. The Mayo Clinic and Johns Hopkins both confirm a small VSD or repaired one without complications poses minimal additional life or pregnancy risk.* Sources: Johns Hopkins Medicine, Mayo Clinic, University of Utah Health. Confidence: high — Universally consistent across major institutional patient education sources.

## Open questions / gaps

- **The brief doesn't know your daughter's specific VSD type (perimembranous vs. muscular vs. subarterial/doubly-committed), exact size in mm, or what the echocardiogram showed about chamber sizes — and these are the variables that actually drive every management decision.** *(Thin research.)* Recommendation: Before the appointment, request the full echocardiogram report from your pediatrician (not just the summary). Bring it. Ask the cardiologist to walk through type, size in mm, location relative to the aortic valve, Qp:Qs estimate, LV chamber dimensions, and pulmonary artery pressure estimate.
- **Most cited research and guidelines focus heavily on infants or adults; data specifically on the natural history of VSDs first detected at the well-child age of 2 is thinner because most VSDs are detected in infancy.** *(Blind spot.)* Recommendation: Ask the cardiologist directly: 'Is it possible this VSD was always there and missed, or could it represent something else (e.g., a VSD that nearly closed leaving an aneurysm, a Gerbode-type LV-to-RA shunt)?' These distinctions change follow-up.
- **The brief has not surfaced the personal/values dimension — how you and the cardiologist will weigh a small lifetime endocarditis risk against the risks of an elective procedure if intervention is offered as 'optional.'** *(Missed consultation.)* Recommendation: If intervention is offered for a defect that doesn't meet guideline criteria, ask explicitly: 'Is this a Class I (recommended), Class IIa (reasonable), or Class IIb (may be considered) indication under AHA/ACC guidelines?' Then ask what would happen if you waited 12 months.
- **Center-volume and surgeon-experience data — which the Pediatric Congenital Heart Association considers central to decision-making — were not researched for your geography.** *(Thin research.)* Recommendation: Use the Conquering CHD Guided Questions Tool (formerly PCHA's). Ask the center for its STS Database outcomes for VSD repair specifically, and ask whether they participate in the IMPACT registry for catheter-based closure.
- **I cannot verify what your specific cardiologist's recommendation will be or their reasoning — the 'when to push back' guidance is general and cannot substitute for the actual clinical conversation.** *(Hallucination.)* Recommendation: Treat the 'push back' framing as a checklist of questions, not a presumption that the cardiologist will overreach. Most pediatric cardiologists for a small VSD at age 2 will recommend observation; if they don't, ask why your child's case differs from the default.

## Recommended next steps

1. Get the full pediatrician's referral note and any echocardiogram report in advance of the appointment, and bring family medical history (cardiac history of siblings, parents, grandparents).
2. Walk in with a written list of questions covering: VSD type and exact size, location relative to the aortic valve, Qp:Qs ratio, LV chamber size, pulmonary artery pressure estimate, current symptoms vs. expected milestones, and follow-up cadence.
3. If intervention is recommended, ask which AHA/ACC indication it meets (Class I/IIa/IIb), what happens if you observe for another 12 months, and request the center's STS Database outcomes for isolated VSD repair.
4. Confirm endocarditis-prophylaxis status in writing (most likely 'not needed') and ask explicitly about dental hygiene expectations and any sport/activity restrictions — the default for small VSDs is none.
5. If anything feels rushed or you don't get clear answers on the items above, get a second opinion at another pediatric congenital heart program before consenting to any procedure — this is explicitly endorsed by Children's Hospital Colorado and the Pediatric Congenital Heart Association.
