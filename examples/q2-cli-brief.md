## Executive summary

Red light therapy for chronic Achilles tendinopathy sits in a frustrating middle zone: biologically plausible, sometimes effective, but not consistently proven. The most rigorous synthesis available — the 2024 Academy of Orthopaedic Physical Therapy Clinical Practice Guideline — concluded low-level laser therapy showed no significant effects on pain or function in midportion Achilles tendinopathy, with low-to-very-low certainty evidence. However, individual high-quality RCTs (notably Stergioulas 2008) using WALT-compliant dosing parameters as an adjunct to eccentric exercise have shown meaningful short-term pain reductions, and dose-stratified meta-analyses suggest a credible 'effective dosage window.' The practical implication: it's a low-risk, low-evidence adjunct to exercise — reasonable to trial for 4–8 weeks if dosing follows WALT guidelines, but exercise (heavy slow resistance or eccentric loading) remains the irreplaceable foundation.

## Key findings

- *The current U.S. clinical practice guideline (2024) does not support routine use of low-level laser therapy for midportion Achilles tendinopathy. A systematic review by Martimbianco et al. of four RCTs (n=119) found the only statistically significant difference at 1 month actually favored the placebo group on the VISA-A functional score, with low-to-very-low certainty of evidence.* Sources: JOSPT 2024 Achilles Tendinopathy Clinical Practice Guideline, Martimbianco et al. 2020 Clin Rehabil. Confidence: high — Authoritative professional society guideline based on systematic review methodology, published 2024.
- *A landmark Level 1 RCT (Stergioulas 2008) found that adding 820 nm laser (5.4 J per session, 6 points along the tendon, 12 sessions over 8 weeks) to eccentric exercises produced significantly lower pain than eccentric exercise plus sham laser at 4, 8, and 12 weeks — the laser group at 4 weeks matched the placebo group's results at 12 weeks, suggesting accelerated recovery.* Sources: Stergioulas et al. 2008 Am J Sports Med. Confidence: high — Single high-quality RCT with clear dose-response design and intention-to-treat analysis; replicated in spirit by Bjordal mechanistic studies.
- *A separate well-designed RCT (Tumilty 2012) using lower laser doses (3 J per point) found no benefit of laser added to eccentric exercise at 4, 12, or 52 weeks — illustrating that dose appears to determine outcome, with sub-WALT dosing producing null results.* Sources: Tumilty et al. 2012 Arch Phys Med Rehabil. Confidence: high — Well-conducted RCT with long-term follow-up; the dose-discrepancy explanation has been debated in the published correspondence.
- *A 2010 systematic review of 25 trials in tendinopathy found 12 positive and 13 null/negative studies, with positive studies clustering around WALT-recommended dosages. Pooled Achilles tendinopathy data showed 13.6 mm pain reduction on a 100 mm VAS — clinically meaningful but based on only two pooled trials.* Sources: Tumilty et al. 2010 Photomed Laser Surg systematic review/meta-analysis. Confidence: medium — Pooled effect from limited number of high-quality trials; dose-window hypothesis is supported but not definitively proven.
- *WALT dose recommendations for Achilles tendinopathy specify 780–860 nm or 904 nm wavelengths, energy doses generally in the 4–8 J/cm² range (typically 4+ Joules per point with multiple points along the tendon), and power density at or below 100 mW/cm² for superficial tendons.* Sources: WALT 2010 dosage recommendations, Bjordal 2012 Photomed Laser Surg, Physiopedia Achilles Tendinopathy Toolkit Section E. Confidence: high — Published consensus guideline from the field's primary professional organization, widely cited in the literature.
- *Across all reviews, no severe adverse events from laser therapy were reported; minor adverse events were typically related to the exercise component (e.g., muscle soreness), not the laser itself.* Sources: JOSPT 2024 Achilles Tendinopathy Clinical Practice Guideline. Confidence: high — Consistent finding across multiple systematic reviews; safety profile is well-established.
- *Eccentric loading (or heavy slow resistance training) remains the standard of care for Achilles tendinopathy, and laser therapy has only ever been studied — and shown benefit — as an adjunct to exercise, never as a standalone treatment.* Sources: JOSPT 2024 Achilles Tendinopathy Clinical Practice Guideline, Stergioulas et al. 2008, Tumilty et al. 2012. Confidence: high — Universal agreement across guidelines, RCTs, and mechanistic literature.

## Open questions / gaps

- **The research does not distinguish between clinic-grade Class 3B/4 lasers (used in nearly all RCTs) and over-the-counter consumer red-light LED panels, which deliver very different power densities and may not reach therapeutic depth.** *(Blind spot.)* Recommendation: Ask your physical therapist what device they use and at what dose; do not assume a home red-light panel replicates the trial conditions. If considering a home device, ask the PT to verify the wavelength (780–860 nm or 904 nm) and irradiance specifications.
- **Most trials studied midportion Achilles tendinopathy specifically; insertional tendinopathy (at the heel bone) was typically excluded and the evidence may not generalize.** *(Thin research.)* Recommendation: Confirm with your PT whether your tendinopathy is midportion or insertional, since insertional cases have different loading protocols (avoiding dorsiflexion past neutral) and the laser evidence base for insertional disease is essentially absent.
- **Long-term outcomes (beyond 12 weeks) are sparse; the one trial with 52-week follow-up was negative, and short-term pain reductions in positive trials may not translate to durable structural tendon changes.** *(Thin research.)* Recommendation: Treat any laser benefit as primarily a short-term pain-management aid that lets you load the tendon more comfortably — not a structural cure. Set a clear stop-point (e.g., 4–8 weeks) to evaluate whether it's helping.
- **The decision of whether laser is worth your time and money depends on factors only you can weigh: cost per session, time burden, whether your insurance covers it, and how severe your symptoms are.** *(Missed consultation.)* Recommendation: Ask your PT for a frank cost-benefit conversation. If laser adds significant cost or time without crowding out your exercise program, the 'low-evidence but low-harm adjunct' framing is reasonable; if it would replace or reduce loading exercises, decline.

## Recommended next steps

1. Tell your PT: 'I'd like to trial low-level laser therapy as an adjunct to my loading program for 4–8 weeks, dosed per WALT 2010 guidelines (780–860 nm or 904 nm wavelength, ~4–8 J per point, 6 points along the tendon, 2–3 sessions per week).' This is the Stergioulas 2008 protocol that produced positive results.
2. Ask the PT to specify the device's wavelength, power output, and per-session joule dose, and to confirm it falls within WALT recommendations — under-dosing is the most common reason laser trials fail.
3. Anchor the plan in eccentric loading (Alfredson protocol or heavy slow resistance) as the primary intervention; treat laser as an adjunct that may speed early-phase pain relief, not as a substitute for loading.
4. Set a pre-defined evaluation point (e.g., week 4 and week 8) using the VISA-A questionnaire and a pain VAS during activity. If you don't see meaningful improvement by week 8, discontinue laser and focus resources on loading and possibly shockwave therapy (which has stronger evidence in some reviews).
5. Confirm whether your tendinopathy is midportion or insertional, since the laser evidence applies to midportion cases and insertional cases need a modified loading approach.

## Sources

- **JOSPT 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline — Midportion Achilles Tendinopathy** — https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2024.0302 (2024). Most authoritative, current professional society guideline; concludes low-level laser therapy is not supported for routine use due to low-quality evidence and lack of consistent benefit. *Note: Highest-tier source: peer-reviewed CPG from the Academy of Orthopaedic Physical Therapy.*
- **Stergioulas et al. 2008 (Am J Sports Med)** — PubMed PMID 18272794; Am J Sports Med 2008;36(5):881-7 (2008). Landmark Level 1 RCT showing 820 nm laser added to eccentric exercise reduced pain at 4, 8, and 12 weeks vs. placebo laser + eccentric exercise. *Note: Single trial; one author (Bjordal) is past president of WALT — disclosed but worth noting.*
- **Tumilty et al. 2012 (Arch Phys Med Rehabil)** — https://www.archives-pmr.org/article/S0003-9993(12)00069-X/abstract (2012). RCT with 52-week follow-up showing no benefit of LLLT (3 J per point) added to eccentric exercise; subject of published correspondence about whether dose was adequate. *Note: High-quality RCT; the dose-adequacy critique by Bjordal is itself contested.*
- **Tumilty et al. 2010 (Photomed Laser Surg) — systematic review and meta-analysis** — PubMed PMID 19708800 (2010). Reviewed 25 trials; identified dose-response window; pooled Achilles data showed 13.6 mm pain reduction on 100 mm VAS. *Note: Now 15+ years old; superseded by more recent reviews.*
- **Martimbianco et al. 2020 (Clin Rehabil) — systematic review and meta-analysis** — PubMed PMID 32204620 (2020). Achilles-specific review concluding evidence is insufficient to support routine LLLT use; certainty of evidence rated low to very low. *Note: Cochrane-style methodology; included in the 2024 CPG.*
- **Tripodi et al. 2021 (BMC Sports Sci Med Rehabil) — systematic review and meta-analysis** — https://bmcsportsscimedrehabil.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13102-021-00306-z (2021). Tendinopathy-wide review noting PBM is frequently used despite lack of high-quality evidence. *Note: PRISMA-compliant; pooled multiple tendinopathy types.*
- **Naterstad/Bjordal et al. 2022 (BMJ Open) — systematic review and meta-analysis** — PubMed PMID 36171024 (2022). Lower-extremity tendinopathy and plantar fasciitis review with WALT-dose subgroup analysis. *Note: Authors are former WALT board members (disclosed); subgroup analysis by dose may be subject to authors' priors.*
- **WALT 2010 Dosage Recommendations** — https://waltpbm.org/documentation-links/recommendations/ (2010 (current as of 2026)). The reference dosing guideline cited across the literature; specifies wavelengths, joule doses, and power densities for tendinopathies. *Note: Professional-society consensus document, not an independent systematic review; useful as a practical parameter reference.*
- **Bjordal 2006 (Br J Sports Med) — activated Achilles tendinitis microdialysis study** — PubMed PMID 16371497 (2006). Mechanistic study showing 904 nm LLLT reduced peritendinous PGE2 (inflammatory marker) and increased pressure pain threshold. *Note: Small (n=7), but provides biological plausibility for the clinical findings.*
- **Physiopedia Achilles Tendinopathy Toolkit — Section E (LLLT Dosage)** — https://www.physio-pedia.com/Achilles_Tendinopathy_Toolkit:_Section_E_-_Low_Level_Laser_Therapy_Dosage_Calculation (Updated periodically). Practical PT-facing summary of WALT dosing for clinical use. *Note: Educational wiki, not peer-reviewed; useful as quick reference, not primary evidence.*
